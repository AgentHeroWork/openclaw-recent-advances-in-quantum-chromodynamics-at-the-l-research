\begin{abstract}
This paper reviews the latest developments in quantum chromodynamics (QCD) as evidenced by experiments conducted at the Large Hadron Collider (LHC). We discuss theoretical advancements, experimental findings, and their implications for understanding the strong force, particle interactions, and the internal structure of protons and neutrons. The analysis includes recent theoretical models, experimental data from high-energy collisions, and the challenges faced in aligning theory with observations. Our findings underscore the importance of continued research in QCD to unravel the complexities of the strong interaction.
\end{abstract}

\section{Analysis}

Comparing theoretical predictions with experimental results from the LHC reveals both successes and limitations of current QCD models. For instance, while lattice QCD has provided accurate predictions for hadron masses, discrepancies remain in the description of dynamic phenomena like jet quenching and the detailed behavior of the quark-gluon plasma. Theoretical models such as the Color Glass Condensate have achieved success in explaining gluon saturation effects but require further refinement to match all observed data \cite{Albacete2014}. Future research in QCD will likely focus on improving the precision of parton distribution functions and developing more comprehensive models that can account for the complex interactions in high-energy collisions. The ongoing analysis of LHC data will continue to challenge and refine our theoretical understanding, suggesting new directions for both experimental and theoretical investigations.

\section{Conclusions}

Recent advances in quantum chromodynamics at the LHC have significantly enriched our understanding of the strong force and particle interactions. The combination of theoretical advancements and experimental discoveries has provided a more detailed picture of the internal structure of protons and neutrons. However, the complexity of QCD ensures that many questions remain unanswered, highlighting the importance of continued research in this field. As the LHC continues to operate and provide new data, we expect further breakthroughs that will deepen our understanding of the universe's fundamental forces. The potential for future discoveries is immense, and the interplay between theory and experiment will remain crucial in unraveling the mysteries of QCD.

\section{Experimental evidence}

The LHC has provided a wealth of experimental data that has significantly advanced our understanding of QCD. High-energy proton-proton collisions have been instrumental in studying jet production and quark-gluon interactions. For example, the CMS and ATLAS collaborations have observed jet quenching, a phenomenon where high-energy partons lose energy when traversing the quark-gluon plasma, indicating the medium's dense and strongly interacting nature \cite{Sirunyan2017, Khachatryan2010}. Heavy-ion collisions at the LHC have provided insights into the properties of the quark-gluon plasma, a state of matter believed to have existed shortly after the Big Bang \cite{Aamodt2010}. Observations of elliptic flow and particle correlations in these collisions suggest the plasma behaves as a nearly perfect fluid, offering new challenges for theoretical models \cite{Gyulassy2005}. These experimental findings not only validate many QCD predictions but also highlight areas where further research is needed to fully understand the strong force's behavior under extreme conditions.

\section{Introduction}

Quantum Chromodynamics (QCD) is a fundamental component of the Standard Model, describing the interactions of quarks and gluons via the strong force. This force is responsible for binding quarks into protons and neutrons, and these nucleons into atomic nuclei. Understanding QCD is crucial for the broader comprehension of particle physics and the universe's underlying structure. The Large Hadron Collider (LHC) at CERN provides an unparalleled facility for probing QCD phenomena at energies previously unattainable in laboratory settings. Its high-energy collisions allow us to test QCD predictions with great precision and explore new phenomena that challenge our theoretical frameworks. This paper aims to provide an overview of recent theoretical and experimental advancements in QCD, emphasizing the role of the LHC in advancing our knowledge of the strong force. We focus on the importance of QCD in explaining strong force interactions and the LHC's pivotal role in testing these theoretical predictions.

\section{Theoretical framework}

Recent theoretical advancements in QCD have focused on phenomena such as gluon saturation, parton distribution functions, and color confinement. These concepts are essential for understanding how quarks and gluons interact at high energies. Gluon saturation models, for instance, predict that at sufficiently high energies, the density of gluons within a proton becomes so large that their mutual interactions start to dominate, leading to a state known as the "Color Glass Condensate" \cite{Balitsky2008}. The evolution of parton distribution functions (PDFs) is another critical area, with new data continuously refining these functions, which describe the momentum distribution of quarks and gluons inside protons \cite{Dulat2016}. Lattice QCD calculations have made significant strides by providing non-perturbative solutions to QCD equations, allowing for precise predictions of hadron masses and interactions \cite{Bazavov2010}. These theoretical developments are crucial for refining our understanding of QCD, though challenges remain, such as the computational complexity and the need for more sophisticated models to describe phenomena observed in experiments.

\begin{abstract}
This paper provides an in-depth analysis of recent advancements in quantum chromodynamics (QCD) facilitated by experiments at the Large Hadron Collider (LHC). It explores theoretical innovations, experimental breakthroughs, and their implications for our understanding of strong interactions. The outcomes of these studies are crucial for refining the Standard Model and exploring phenomena beyond it. By integrating both theoretical insights and experimental results, this paper offers a holistic view of the current state of research in QCD, highlighting the precision of QCD calculations, the challenges faced, and the potential for future discoveries beyond the Standard Model.
\end{abstract}

\section{Analysis}

The comparison between theoretical predictions and experimental results at the LHC has led to significant advances in our understanding of QCD. The precision of QCD calculations, particularly those involving higher-order perturbative corrections, has improved, allowing for more accurate cross-section predictions. These calculations are essential for interpreting LHC data and searching for new physics beyond the Standard Model.

One of the ongoing challenges in QCD is the accurate determination of parton distribution functions (PDFs), which are crucial for predicting outcomes of proton-proton collisions. The improved precision of PDFs, informed by LHC data, has reduced uncertainties in theoretical predictions, enhancing the reliability of QCD as a tool for understanding particle interactions.

Despite these advances, several unresolved questions remain, such as the nature of confinement and the detailed dynamics of the quark-gluon plasma. These challenges highlight the need for further theoretical and experimental efforts to deepen our understanding of QCD and its implications for the fundamental structure of matter.

\section{Conclusions}

The recent advances in quantum chromodynamics, driven by experimental results from the LHC, have significantly enhanced our understanding of the strong interaction. Theoretical innovations, such as improved lattice QCD calculations and refined perturbative techniques, have provided a deeper insight into the behavior of quarks and gluons. Experimental breakthroughs, including detailed studies of jet physics and the quark-gluon plasma, have validated and challenged existing models, prompting further refinements.

These developments have important implications for the future of particle physics, particularly in the search for phenomena beyond the Standard Model. The precision of QCD calculations and the discovery of new hadronic states suggest promising directions for future research. As the LHC continues to operate at higher energies and luminosities, it is expected to yield further insights into the fundamental nature of strong interactions and the potential for new discoveries.

\section{Experimental evidence}

The LHC experiments have been pivotal in testing QCD predictions. ATLAS and CMS, the two largest general-purpose detectors, have provided extensive data on jet physics, which is essential for understanding QCD processes at high energies. Jets are collimated streams of particles resulting from the hadronization of quarks and gluons, and their study helps elucidate the dynamics of the strong force.

Heavy ion collisions, studied extensively by the ALICE experiment, recreate conditions similar to those just after the Big Bang, allowing the exploration of the quark-gluon plasma (QGP), a state of matter where quarks and gluons are no longer confined within hadrons. Observations of QGP properties, such as temperature and viscosity, have provided vital insights into QCD under extreme conditions.

Recent experimental results include precise measurements of the strong coupling constant $\alpha_s$, which is crucial for testing the running of the coupling predicted by QCD. Additionally, the discovery of new hadronic states and exotic particles, such as tetraquarks and pentaquarks, has challenged traditional quark models and prompted new theoretical developments.

\section{Introduction}

Quantum Chromodynamics (QCD) is the theory of the strong interaction, one of the four fundamental forces in nature, which describes the interactions between quarks and gluons, the fundamental constituents of protons and neutrons. As a crucial component of the Standard Model of particle physics, QCD is responsible for the binding of quarks into protons, neutrons, and other hadrons, which collectively form the atomic nuclei. The theory is characterized by the property of confinement, whereby quarks and gluons are never found in isolation, and asymptotic freedom, which allows quarks to behave as free particles at high energies.

The Large Hadron Collider (LHC), located at CERN near Geneva, Switzerland, is the world's largest and most powerful particle accelerator. It provides an unprecedented environment for testing QCD by colliding protons at energies up to 13 TeV, enabling the study of strong interactions at scales never before accessible. With its four main experiments—ATLAS, CMS, ALICE, and LHCb—the LHC has significantly advanced our understanding of QCD, offering insights into phenomena such as jet formation, heavy ion collisions, and the production of exotic states of matter.

This paper aims to review the recent theoretical and experimental advancements in QCD facilitated by the LHC. It discusses the fundamental principles of QCD, explores the latest theoretical models, and presents key experimental results. The analysis provides a critical assessment of how these developments have refined our understanding of the strong force and their implications for the future of particle physics research.

\section{Theoretical framework}

Quantum Chromodynamics is founded on the concept of color charge, analogous to electric charge in electromagnetism, but with three types: red, green, and blue. Quarks, which carry color charge, interact by exchanging gluons, the force carriers. The QCD Lagrangian, which describes these interactions, is given by:

$$
\mathcal{L}_{\text{QCD}} = \sum_{f}\bar{\psi}_f(i\gamma^{\mu}D_{\mu} - m_f)\psi_f - \frac{1}{4}G^{a}_{\mu\nu}G^{\mu\nu}_a,
$$

where $\psi_f$ represents the quark fields of flavor $f$, $D_{\mu}$ is the covariant derivative, $m_f$ are the quark masses, and $G^{a}_{\mu\nu}$ is the gluon field strength tensor.

Recent theoretical advances in QCD include lattice QCD and perturbative QCD. Lattice QCD is a non-perturbative approach that discretizes spacetime on a lattice, allowing numerical simulations of QCD processes. It has been instrumental in calculating hadron masses and decay constants with remarkable precision. Perturbative QCD, on the other hand, is applicable at high energies where the strong coupling constant $\alpha_s$ becomes small, enabling a series expansion in terms of $\alpha_s$. This approach has been crucial for predicting cross-sections at the LHC.

The LHC has provided critical data that has necessitated refinements in QCD models. For example, studies of jet production and the behavior of the strong coupling constant at high energies have led to more accurate parton distribution functions (PDFs), which describe the momentum distribution of quarks and gluons inside the proton. These improvements are vital for making precise predictions for LHC experiments.
